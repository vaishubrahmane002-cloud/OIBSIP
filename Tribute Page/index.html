Kalpana Chawla

The First Indian Woman in Space 🚀

About

Kalpana Chawla was an Indian-American astronaut and aerospace engineer who became the first woman of Indian origin to travel to space. She was born on March 17, 1962, in Karnal, Haryana, India. From a young age, she was fascinated by airplanes and the mysteries of space, which inspired her to pursue a career in aerospace engineering.

She completed her Bachelor’s degree in Aeronautical Engineering from Punjab Engineering College in India and later moved to the United States to continue her studies. Kalpana earned a Master’s degree in Aerospace Engineering from the University of Texas and a Ph.D. from the University of Colorado.

In 1994, she was selected as an astronaut by NASA. She first flew into space in 1997 on the Space Shuttle mission STS-87, where she served as a mission specialist. During her career, she spent over 30 days in space conducting scientific experiments and research. Her second mission was on the Space Shuttle STS-107 in 2003, dedicated to scientific research and experiments. Tragically, the spacecraft disintegrated during re-entry into Earth’s atmosphere, and the entire crew lost their lives.

Despite the tragedy, Kalpana Chawla’s legacy continues to inspire millions of people around the world. She is remembered for her courage, determination, and belief that dreams can become reality through hard work and dedication. Her journey from a small town in India to the vastness of space remains a powerful symbol of inspiration, especially for young women who aspire to pursue careers in science, technology, and exploration.

Life Timeline

  • March 17, 1962: Born in Karnal, Haryana, India.
  • 1982: Graduated in Aeronautical Engineering from Punjab Engineering College.
  • 1988: Earned her Ph.D. in Aerospace Engineering from the University of Colorado.
  • 1994: Selected by NASA as an astronaut candidate out of thousands of applicants.
  • 1997: Made history as the first woman of Indian origin in space aboard Space Shuttle Columbia (STS-87).
  • Feb 1, 2003: Tragically lost her life when Space Shuttle Columbia disintegrated upon re-entry (STS-107).
